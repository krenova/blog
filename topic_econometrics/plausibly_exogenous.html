The foundations of the 2 stage least squares (2SlS) method lies on the ability to find a valid instrument. Consider the following 2 stage model, \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} y &= \beta^\intercal x_1 + \delta x_2 + \epsilon \\ x_2 &= \pi^\intercal x_1 + \theta z + \eta. \end{aligned} \end{equation} A valid instrument, \(z\), is defined to be correlated to \(y\) only through \(x_2\). A simple statement but it has many consequences. Common intepretations of this are

  1. \(\mathbb{E}[z\epsilon] = 0 \), covariance of \(z\) and 2nd stage error term \(\epsilon\) is 0.
  2. \( \theta\ne 0 \), \(x_2\) must be explained by \(z\).
When an instrument satisfies the above properties, it is also said to satisfy the exclusion restriction requirements.

A lesser known but equivalent interpretation defined by Conley et al (2011) is that when the following equivalent 2 stage model is evaluated \begin{equation} \begin{aligned} y &= \beta^\intercal x_1 + \delta x_2 + \gamma z + \epsilon \\ x_2 &= \pi^\intercal x_1 + \theta z + \eta, \end{aligned} \end{equation} the exclusion restriction of \(z\) is fulfilled when \(\gamma = 0\). Note that this model clearly cannot be evaluated through the conventional frequentist approach due to collinearity arising from \(z\) appearing in both the 1st and 2nd stage models but has to estimated through Bayesian methods or other approaches as suggested in Conley et al (2011). More importantly however, this definition allows us to relax the exclusion restriction requirement and instead, allow for that possibility that \(\gamma\ne 0 \) but is sufficiently small such that the estimate of \(\delta\), while no longer unbiased, is sufficiently accurate for practical use or meaningful interpretation. The question is how do we quantify sufficiently small?

In the simple case where \(x_1\) in the models above are not considered, Conley et al (2011) shows that \begin{equation} \hat{\delta} \rightarrow \delta + \gamma/\theta. \end{equation} Hence, when \(\theta\) is large, we have a larger buffer against bias due to \(\gamma\ne 0\) and vice versa. In Conley's paper, he and his authors further developed this idea to define confidence intervals for estimates of \(\hat{\delta}\) under the possibility that the exclusion restriction requirement is mildly violated.
